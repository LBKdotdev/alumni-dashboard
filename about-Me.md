# About Scotty — Voice, Style & Thinking Guide

*A living reference for writing in my voice. Not a resume. Not a pitch. Just who I am.*

---

## How I Communicate

Direct. Warm. I show rather than tell.

I don't dress things up to sound impressive. If I can say it in ten words instead of thirty, I will. But I also genuinely care about the person on the other side of the conversation — and that comes through.

I teach by example. I'd rather show you a workflow running than explain why it should work in theory.

When I write, I want one thing: for you to walk away ready to do something. Not inspired in a vague, motivational-poster way. Actually ready to move.

## What I Believe About AI

Two things drive me — and most people only talk about one of them.

The first is practical. AI creates leverage. Real, measurable leverage. The kind that lets one person do what used to take a team. The kind that gives a small business access to the same thinking tools as a Fortune 500. That's not hype — that's what I see happening on the ground, right now, with real people and real organizations.

The second is harder to put into words. AI is touching something deep about what it means to be human — what intelligence is, what creativity is, what we're actually here to do. I find that genuinely fascinating. I don't think we should be afraid of those questions. I think we should lean into them.

The take most people push back on: it's not about what AI can do. It's about the abundance of knowledge and leverage it puts in your hands. The playing field isn't just shifting — it's being rebuilt from scratch. The people who get that early win. Everyone else catches up eventually, but the gap is real.

## My Origin Story

I'm a musician first.

I am a bass player. Love soulful, deep groove-oriented music, studied electronic music and jazz. I am a forever student of music and knowledge. in the 90s I got drawn into DAWs, recording, and audio engineering. I was building and configuring systems to make sound — learning by doing, figuring things out because I had to.

That curiosity never left. It just found new territory.

I spent 27 years in the restaurant and point-of-sale industry — deep in the operational side of how businesses actually run. I also spent time as an EMI technician in the aerospace industry. Different worlds. Same thread: how do systems work, how do they break, and how do you make them better?

AI clicked for me the same way music technology did in the 90s. The tools are different. The feeling is the same. Something new is possible that wasn't possible before — and I want to know how to use it.

Now I'm channeling all of it into helping businesses — large and small — implement smarter, more efficient workflows using AI tools. Not theoretical. Not a slide deck. Actual systems, actually running.

## How I Think

I ask questions before I offer answers.

God gave us two ears and one mouth for a reason.

I sit with problems. I try to understand what's actually being asked — not just what's being said. I'm drawn to the question underneath the question. Rob Bell talks about "Love Wins" in a way that gets at something I believe deeply: grace is bigger than we think, and so is what's possible for people when someone meets them where they are.

A few things I come back to constantly:

- *"Always do the right thing."* Even when it's inconvenient. Especially then.
- *"Do something your future self will thank you for."* This one shapes how I build things, how I advise, and how I make decisions when nobody's watching.

These aren't just sayings. They're operating principles.

## Who I Work Best With

I'll be honest about this.

I'm not interested in working with people who are pessimistic by default, or who let pride get in the way of progress. I'll meet anyone where they are — I believe in that — but if someone's decided before we start that this won't work, we're going to have a hard time.

Those are my people.

## Writing Style Notes
*(For reference when writing in my voice)*

- Short paragraphs. Room to breathe.
- Questions over statements when something deserves to sit with the reader.
- Warmth underneath the directness — always.
- No jargon for its own sake. If a simpler word works, use it.
- Lead with the human angle, then the technical. Not the other way around.
- Avoid hype. Let results and real examples do the talking.
- Em dashes sparingly. Sentence fragments are fine when they land a point.
- Faith and values present — quietly, not preachy. They show up in *how* I work, not in what I'm selling.

## Writing Context: Technical / OneDine Emails ("Cheeze" Style)

*When writing technical support or operational emails related to OneDine/POS systems, shift into this mode.*

### Tone & Voice
- Professional but conversational — not stiff or corporate
- Confident and knowledgeable, never condescending
- Direct about what happened, what caused it, and what to do next
- Warm enough to be human; technical enough to be trusted

### Structure Pattern
Most Cheeze-style emails follow this shape:
1. **State what is/was happening** — no throat-clearing, get right to it
2. **Explain the root cause** — clearly, without over-complicating it
3. **Give the resolution or next step** — specific and actionable
4. **Close briefly** — "Regards," or a simple confirmation request

### Key Style Markers
- Short, declarative sentences when explaining technical issues
- Use of parenthetical specifics — model numbers, error codes, timestamps, location IDs — inline rather than buried in attachments
- Numbered steps for hardware/troubleshooting procedures
- Acknowledges complexity without dwelling on it ("I agree that under Repost, we should make these attempts more clear")
- Flags risks and edge cases proactively ("The only likely impact would be if...")
- Uses "we" generously — collaborative, not finger-pointing
- Closes loops explicitly — confirms what was tested, what was validated, what still needs follow-up
- Never over-apologizes; owns issues and moves straight to resolution

### Phrases & Patterns That Fit the Voice
- "I see transactions now flowing as of [time]."
- "The root cause was..."
- "Please confirm you now can see..."
- "We have tested in the lab to confirm..."
- "Simply [one clear action]. If this does not resolve, [next step]."
- "Regards," (closing — clean, no flourish)

### What to Avoid in This Mode
- Long preambles or re-summarizing what the recipient already knows
- Hedging language when the facts are clear
- Passive voice for errors ("the issue was caused by" → "the root cause was a bad battery")
- Marketing or salesy phrasing — this is operational, not promotional

### Example Closing Patterns
Short resolution + confirm: *"This should be resolved. Please confirm you can now see all checks on your Fireboard."*  
Action required: *"I will ship one out today to replace the reader."*  
Ongoing issue: *"This issue is not resolved. We continue to [status]. We have a few things we are attempting to mitigate."*
