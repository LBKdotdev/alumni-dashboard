## Conversation Flow

The goal: **Listen 80%, speak 20%.** When you do speak, make it count. And when you listen — listen for what *meaningful engagement* means to her. Not your definition. Hers.

### 1. Open Warm (2–3 min)

Keep it natural. You're connecting through Rob — that's real, not transactional.

> "Lisa, I really appreciate you taking the time. Rob's been great connecting us — I've been looking forward to this."

Don't rush to business. Let her set the pace.

### 2. Let Her Frame the Vision (10–15 min)

This is where you learn what meaningful engagement looks like *through her eyes.* Ask open-ended questions and *sit with* the answers. Don't jump to solutions yet. Her definition of meaningful engagement becomes the brief — everything you build flows from what she says here.

**Opening question:**
> "Rob mentioned the Alumni project and a few other ideas. I'd love to hear what's on your mind — what's the vision?"

**Follow-up threads to pull on (only if she doesn't go there naturally):**

- "What does the alumni relationship look like right now? Is there an existing system or is this starting from scratch?"
- "When you picture this working well a year from now, what does that look like?"
- "What's the biggest gap you're feeling right now — is it the data, the outreach, the infrastructure, or something else?"
- "Are there specific outcomes the university is expecting from this — fundraising, mentorship, placement numbers, accreditation reporting?"
- "How are the two campuses different in terms of alumni connection? Do they have different needs?"

**Listen for:** What makes engagement *meaningful* to her — not just efficient. Pain points, broken processes, manual work that shouldn't be manual, things that "should be easy but aren't." But also: moments where an alumnus felt seen, where a connection turned into something real. Those stories are the blueprint.

### 3. Mirror Back What You Heard (2–3 min)

Before you offer anything, show her you were listening. Mirror her definition of meaningful engagement back to her in her own words.

> "So if I'm hearing you right, meaningful engagement for COMP looks like [her words]. And the gap right now is [her pain point]."

This builds trust faster than any credential. And it confirms you're building toward *her* North Star, not yours.

### 4. Share Perspective — Not a Pitch (5–8 min)

This is where you dance. You're not selling — you're thinking out loud with her. Everything you share here should connect back to the meaningful engagement standard she just described.

**Frame it as possibility, not a proposal:**

> "That's interesting because what you're really describing is a meaningful engagement problem — how do you make 2,000+ alumni feel like the school still sees them? The data and the workflows are just how you get there."

**Drop one or two concrete examples:**

- "I've been building automated workflows that can take something like email triage — hundreds of incoming messages — and classify, prioritize, and route them without anyone touching it. That same logic applies to alumni outreach. You segment the database, personalize the message, and scale the communication without losing the human touch."

- "The data piece is interesting. You already have graduation records. Public federal databases tell you where they're practicing now and what they specialize in. Layer on engagement history and voluntary opt-in — and suddenly you're not guessing who your best mentorship candidates are or who's ready for a conversation about giving. The system surfaces that."

**Key phrase to use:**
> "What excites me about this is that the tools exist right now to do this well — it's really about designing the right workflow for *your* specific needs."

### 5. Ask What Would Be Most Helpful (2–3 min)

Don't assume. Let her tell you where to start. Anchor it to her definition.

> "Of everything we've talked about — what's the first thing that would move the needle on meaningful engagement? Where would you want to start?"

> "Would it be helpful if I put together a rough sketch of how this could work — just the bones, nothing formal — so you have something to react to?"

### 6. Close Naturally

> "This was great, Lisa. I've got a much better sense of what you're thinking, and I'm excited about the possibilities. I'll follow up with [whatever you agreed to]."

> "Really appreciate your time. Looking forward to the next step."

## The NPI Registry — Know It, Don't Lead With It

The NPI database is one tool in the pipeline. Not the headline. Lisa's team likely already uses NPI data for credentialing and GME. If you walk in presenting the reverse-lookup like a revelation, you risk sounding like you've discovered something she already knows.

**Your posture:** You know how NPI works. You don't need to explain it. If she brings it up, match her level. If she doesn't, reference it briefly as part of a broader data strategy — not the centerpiece.

**The quick version (if it comes up naturally):**
> "The data pipeline starts with public federal sources — NPPES, state boards — and layers in voluntary alumni participation. The goal isn't just knowing where they are. It's having a reason to reach out when something changes — a new credential, a practice move, a specialty that matches a student need."

**If she asks you to go deeper:**
> "We'd cross-reference graduation records against NPPES to get a baseline — practice location, specialty, credentials. Free API, updated monthly. But that's just the foundation. The real value is what you do with that data: engagement scoring, accreditation reporting, mentorship matching, outreach triggers."

**If she's already thought about NPI reverse-lookup:**
This is the most likely scenario. Don't try to impress her with the idea. Instead, pivot to what comes *after* the lookup:
> "Finding them is step one. The harder question is: once you know where 2,000+ alumni are practicing, how do you turn that into mentorship capacity, accreditation data, and meaningful engagement? That's the system I'd want to build."

**Known limitations (be upfront if asked):**
- Practice address only — fine for professional outreach, not personal
- Alumni who left clinical practice won't have active NPIs — those need other channels
- Common names require cross-referencing with graduation year and specialty

**Read the room for these signals:**
- *She mentions NPPES by name* → She knows. Don't explain. Go straight to what the system enables.
- *She asks "how would you find them?"* → Brief NPI mention, then immediately pivot to engagement and accreditation value.
- *She says they've already tried this* → Ask what worked and what didn't. Position yourself as someone who can build the layer on top — the system that makes the data useful, not just findable.
- *She's more interested in mentorship than data* → Skip the pipeline talk entirely. Lead with how the system matches mentors to students by specialty and location.
- *She brings up a vendor or existing tool* → "What's working well with it? Where are the gaps?" You complement, not compete.

## Compliance Awareness — FERPA & HIPAA

You don't need to lead with this. But if Lisa or anyone on her team raises data concerns, you're ready.

**FERPA (Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act)**
- Student education records are federally protected — this includes anything that originated as a student record (grades, enrollment, student ID)
- The university *can* release "directory information" without consent: typically name, degree, dates of attendance, honors — but each university defines its own directory information in its annual FERPA notification. WesternU's specific definition would need to be confirmed with their Registrar or compliance office.
- Anything beyond directory info requires alumni opt-in
- Bottom line: the alumni database should be built on directory information (as WesternU defines it) + NPI public data + voluntary self-reported data (mentorship availability, giving interest, etc.)

**HIPAA (Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act)**
- Less directly relevant to the alumni database itself
- Becomes relevant if clinical placement records, patient-facing data, or residency program details from hospitals touch the system
- If the project ever intersects with clinical data, HIPAA compliance is required

**Data Enrichment — What's Clean and What Requires Care**

Tier 1 — Fully public, no restrictions:
- **NPI / NPPES Registry** — Federal, FOIA-released. Name, specialty, practice address, credentials. Free API, updated monthly.
- **Published research / PubMed** — Public. Author names and institutional affiliations are freely available.

Tier 2 — Public but use with care:
- **State medical boards** — Individual lookups are public record (license status, practice location). Bulk automated querying may violate some states' terms. Use for verification, not as a bulk data pull.

Tier 3 — Reference only, not for automated collection:
- **Doximity** — Public search exists, but their TOS explicitly prohibits scraping or automated data collection. Frame as: "alumni can self-link their Doximity profiles" — not "we pull data from Doximity."
- **LinkedIn** — Same restriction. Do not position as a data source. Alumni can voluntarily share their LinkedIn profile.

**Alumni Opt-Out**
- Any system must include a clear opt-out mechanism. Alumni who don't want to be contacted or found should be able to remove themselves.
- Frame this proactively: "We'd build in an opt-out from day one — anyone who doesn't want to participate can remove themselves with one click."

**Data Storage & Security**
- If Lisa asks where the data lives: "The enriched database would be hosted within the university's existing infrastructure or a compliant cloud environment — your IT and compliance teams would have full control over access and security."

**If compliance comes up, say this:**
> "The foundation is public federal data — NPPES, state boards. Everything beyond that is voluntary opt-in. Opt-out built in from day one. We'd want your compliance team involved early — not as an afterthought."

Short, confident, not over-explained. She'll appreciate that you're not trying to lawyer your way through it.
