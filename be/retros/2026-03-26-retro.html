What Went Well

Went Well

Speed without chaos

7 versions in one day. Every version shipped clean — committed, pushed, build report written, index updated. No regressions. No rollbacks needed. The Be chain reaction (work, review, report, index, commit, push) prevented the usual "fast = sloppy" trap. Structure enabled speed, didn't slow it down.

Went Well

Catching the creep factor early

The meeting transcription quotes ("Good morning, Dr. Warren" greeting, "I was in your shoes" SOAP quote) could have shipped to Lisa and felt invasive. Flagged early, replaced with something better (mission briefing, stats-only SOAP card). The instinct to ask "would this feel creepy?" saved the demo.

Went Well

Developer language audit

"Scraping" and "Apify Pipeline" would have undermined trust instantly. Renaming to "Practice Intelligence Layer" reframes a technical capability as a product feature. Same data, completely different perception. This kind of naming discipline is the difference between a prototype and a product.

Went Well

The PubMed fix was surgical

5,795 inflated publication counts fixed at the rendering layer with one function. Data preserved for future disambiguation. Zero trust risk. The decision to go qualitative ("Published researcher") instead of capped ("Published ~50 papers") was the right call — even low counts are suspect with common names.

Went Well

Design instincts leveled up the product

The Rolodex animation, deep burgundy sidebar, and login gate weren't in any spec. They came from creative conversation. Each one made the prototype feel more intentional, more owned, more "this was built for us." The NaciLink branding conversation is the natural next step — the product is earning a name.

What Could Be Better

Improve

PubMed counts should have been caught in v1.0

The inflated publication numbers (350,828 papers for one person) were in the data from day one. They survived 4 boards, 5 QA passes, and 4 ship cycles before being flagged in the review document. The QA passes tested functionality (does the card render?) but not data sanity (does the number make sense?). Future QA should include a "would Lisa believe this?" smell test on displayed data.

Improve

The review document should have existed earlier

The prototype review (12 categories, checklists, fixes) was written after v1.4. It should have been the FIRST artifact — grade against the meeting brief, then build. Instead, the review retroactively validated work that was already done. Building the scorecard first would have caught the PubMed issue, the developer language, and the meeting quote sensitivity before they shipped.

Improve

Versioning cadence was too fast for meaningful QA

Seven versions in one day means each version got minimal soak time. The ship cycle worked (nothing broke), but there was no space for "live with it for a day and see what feels wrong." The burgundy sidebar, for example, shipped and immediately felt right — but the Rolodex animation hasn't had real user feedback yet. Consider batching cosmetic refinements into fewer, more deliberate releases.

Improve

The "wow moment" suggestion is still unbuilt

Pre-populating Lisa's actual handwritten notes into a profile was identified as the single highest-impact demo moment. It's been in the review doc since v1.5 and still hasn't been implemented. It's a 5-minute task that changes the entire emotional register of the demo. Should have been prioritized over cosmetic work.

Commitments

Commitment #1

Build the "wow moment" before the next demo

Pre-populate 1-2 profiles with Lisa's actual words from the meeting transcript. Date and attribute to "Lisa Warren." This is the single highest-ROI task remaining.

Commitment #2

QA includes a "would Lisa believe this?" data pass

Every QA cycle should include a sanity check on displayed numbers, names, and language. Not just "does it render" but "does it make sense to a non-technical dean." The PubMed lesson applies broadly.

Commitment #3

Grade before you build

Next project: write the review scorecard FIRST, against the discovery meeting brief. Then build to the scorecard. This inverts the current flow (build, then validate) into a tighter loop (define success, then build to it).

Commitment #4

NaciLink branding decision

The product is earning a name. Decide on NaciLink (or alternative), source a clean Vitruvian Man image (public domain, not Google watermark), and integrate the brand into the login gate, nav, and favicon. This turns "the prototype" into a product with identity.
